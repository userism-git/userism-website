Amid robots, artificial intelligence, and smartphones, we often ask ourselves: Where are we heading, and what will become of us?
The answer is simple: We will all become users, labor will matter less and less, and we will have to compete with those who control the flow of our data.
These are the consequences of digitalization, an unstoppable process that can either become a positive experience for everyone or remain a lever of power for the few.
This article will address the issue starting from labor, then moving on to consumption and the rise of tech companies.
In closing, it will introduce a new political vision called "userism," which aims to redistribute digital capital into the hands of the people.

The Neolithic Ends in Iowa

To understand the meaning of digitalization, it is useful to start from Iowa and its endless fields of corn and soybeans.
Specifically, let’s follow the life of David Miller, an ideal-typical farmer, during a typical workday.
It is 2026, and he is one of many farmers populating his state.
He works with a couple of colleagues on a medium-sized field, less than a square kilometer.
The weather is nice, and David begins his morning by checking his phone to see which parts of the land have been sown, irrigated, fertilized, and so on.
The computer systems confirm it is time to harvest part of the crop, so he and his colleagues board their agricultural machinery.
With the combine harvester, they follow invisible but precise lines thanks to the satellite and digital systems integrated into the vehicle.
Their coordination is flawless.

In Iowa, corn and soybeans are traditionally grown, but David is diversifying his field production by implementing new seeds and strategies.
Yet, no radical changes to his means of production are needed: the software he uses adapts to every decision, helping him understand how to operate the machinery to accommodate new crops.
One way or another, he always finds himself dealing with touchscreens and control panels, guided in every decision—whether driving, selling the harvest, or buying new seeds and fertilizers.
After a day’s work, David is tired and returns home, spending time with his family and checking his phone to see what’s happening in the world.
He feels proud.
In just one day of work, he has fed hundreds of people, both inside and outside the country.
It is thanks to farmers like him that the United States is now the world’s third-largest agricultural producer and the top exporter.
Yet, the entire U.S. primary sector employs only 1.6% of the national workforce—about one person for every 130.
This is the complete opposite of what our ancestors were used to.

Since the Neolithic era, the vast majority of humanity has lived by farming, sustaining a small elite of non-farmers.
To our ancestors, someone like David Miller might have seemed like a saint, sent to free arms from the fields and heads from the sun, allowing everyone to eat without having to work.
For David Miller and all his fellow citizens, however, this is normality.
The great peasant masses disappear from the face of technological societies, replaced by a few indispensable workers empowered by a dense network of digital interconnections.

Robots Threaten Zhengzhou

On the other side of the world, digitalization advances relentlessly.
We are in China, where vast factories tirelessly produce goods for the world—not food, but products of all kinds, from smartphones to cars to sneakers.
Zhang Jing, an ideal-typical Chinese worker, has lived in Zhengzhou for several years.
Every morning, she goes to a Foxconn plant (a Taiwanese company) famous for assembling most of the iPhones sold globally.
Her job is still labor-intensive because Apple does not trust machines for this delicate phase.
Workers are more precise, so an assembly line like the one Zhang Jing works on consists of more than a thousand people.
In the Zhengzhou plant, there are nearly a hundred lines working in parallel.

Talking with her colleagues, Jing learns that Foxconn’s upper management is testing robots to halve the number of employees needed for each line.
These are sophisticated machines that must be capable of extremely precise movements and perfect coordination, as well as performing functional tests.
A deep fusion of digital and mechanical.
The fact is that assembling a standard Android phone requires only a few dozen people, and if robots could guarantee a quality level worthy of Apple, there would be nothing wrong with cutting a few jobs.
In fact, the Cupertino giant has explicitly asked Foxconn to implement greater automation after protests and violent clashes between workers and police in 2022 reminded everyone that concentrating a large workforce can become a political problem.
When Zhang Jing returns home, she is worried.
If she loses her job due to the robots Apple wants, she is not sure what she could do next.
As she falls asleep, she thinks about what it would be like to work in the delivery sector—running around a minimarket at the beck and call of an app, standing all day.
It would not be an improvement.
And who can guarantee that they won’t invent a robot to do that too?

The Flight of Labor

It is easy to empathize with Zhang Jing.
After all, in the West, we have lost millions of jobs in the secondary sector over a few decades, so we instinctively understand what a sudden cut in employment might mean.
We are well aware of the great flight of manual labor to the East, escaping the hands of the organized and well-paid working masses of the West.
In hindsight, the flight of labor from the EU and the U.S. has weakened the working masses, throwing the left into a chronic crisis and thus allowing elites to dismantle labor protections and redistribution systems starting in the 1980s.
A good portion of these fleeing jobs took refuge in China, a country that today enjoys industrial production greater than that of the United States, Germany, Japan, and India combined.

Yet something incredible is happening: in China, labor has not found a home but something more akin to a hospital bed to keep it alive a little longer.
Despite the Chinese secondary sector’s growth rate stabilizing around 5% annually, the number of employed people has declined in both absolute and relative terms after peaking in 2010.
Not because production is fleeing to other countries like India or Vietnam, but because China is robotizing at such a speed that it allows production to grow at sustained rates while reducing the number of workers needed.

Today, about 32% of Chinese workers are employed in the secondary sector, but this percentage may never reach the levels typical of the first industrialized countries.
In terms of automation, however, the trend is clear.
In 2018, China ranked 19th in the world for robot density in industry.
Within five years, it climbed 16 positions to become third, behind only South Korea and Singapore.
So, just like our farmer David Miller, workers like Zhang Jing could one day be part of a small minority.

Tech Societies: A Shared Experience

From the stories of David Miller and Zhang Jing, we understand something about our future: neither agriculture nor industry is central in determining the structure of societies.
With all due respect to Marx and Mao, the times of communist revolutions led by workers and peasants seem to have ended, not so much due to a lack of will but to a lack of organic unity.
The solidarity that once arose from sharing workspaces and conditions does not replicate in the tertiary sector, which is characterized by increasing complexity and fragmentation of tasks—tasks that are also subject to automation and constantly transformed by digitalization.
On these bases, it is difficult to establish a collective movement founded on labor, with a solid theory and clear political direction.

Digitalization, on the other hand, constitutes a truly shared and systematic experience in the lives of people in the new millennium.
It permeates, integrates, and replaces all traditional spaces of interaction, crossing social classes and national borders.
It is not a phenomenon attributable to a single culture or place of innovation, because it arises and spreads thanks to the human tendency toward abstraction—that is, to create symbolic worlds that accompany and sometimes replace direct interaction with material reality.
Therefore, every digital gesture, the most common of which is using a smartphone, arises from a deeply natural need that involves every person.

Moreover, in tech societies, all users become an involuntary source of data, regardless of their job or social role.
The surrounding human environment thus develops systems for data extraction (often for profit or control) that inevitably unite users as objects of the same exploitation.

Data Asymmetry

Tech societies stand on an economic system that is more efficient and convenient than any other in history, characterized by:

  • The growing importance of users, who, by inhabiting the digital space, generate both the demand for goods and services and the data used to create and improve their supply;
  • The reduction of human workers needed to produce and distribute goods and services, increasing the time available for digital consumption;
  • The rise of those companies or governments that manage to obtain or impose their control over digital capital—that is, data and the technologies to extract and use it.
Faced with these transformations, it is natural for those who fail to interpret them and suffer their consequences to feel disoriented.
We can call them, in an analytical sense, the users of digitalization.
On the other side are the actors of digitalization: large tech companies and some governments that design, accelerate, and direct change.

The asymmetry between users and actors stems from a very different understanding of computer systems.
For the former, they are services to use, to increase their satisfaction and productivity.
For the latter, they are means of data extraction, where the final service is often a pretext for probing various aspects of human behavior.

All Power to the Users!

In this article, I propose starting with the users themselves to correct the paradox of asymmetry and structure fairer tech societies.
To do this, we must awaken in users the awareness that they are the true core of digital wealth.
Awakening the self-awareness of the digital people is a step toward enabling the formation of collective organizations that could counterbalance the power of digital capital holders.
This idea, as a whole, is what I call "userism" (from "user").

Userism sees the birth of the society of users as an opportunity to redistribute digital capital.
It is not about containing with rules, taxes, or limits the extraction of data and value carried out by platforms, nor about patching up an occupational crisis that is now structural.
These interventions remain palliative.

Userism instead proposes to appropriate the rivers of data that fuel the digital economy, channeling them toward collective benefit.
For this to happen, however, the connected user community must assume political control over the scope and direction of these flows.

If the community were to govern the digital transition, three outcomes would become natural:

  • Data as a common good;
  • Automation as a tool for liberation;
  • Individual protection (privacy and more) as a standard.
Imagine the collective well-being that would arise if Amazon’s algorithms were used to efficiently distribute goods worldwide, without translating into unfair dividends and hyper-consumerism?
If the deep knowledge of the human soul possessed by Google or OpenAI were used to improve services and well-being, without addiction processes or surveillance strategies?
If the productive, managerial, medical, and logistical capabilities of these systems were made available for the benefit of all, not the profit of a few?

Userism proposes to pursue this vision by promoting awareness of the power already present in users.
Their strength comes from two objective and undeniable factors:

  • The critical mass they represent;
  • The essentiality of their social role within the digital ecosystem.
Once aware, they could leverage their role to determine the ownership of digital capital itself.
At this point, the only missing element would be imagination: the ability to design the mechanisms through which to translate this awareness into concrete tools, thus securing the benefits of digitalization.
